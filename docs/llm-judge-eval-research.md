# LLM-as-a-Judge for Autocomplete Evaluation: Research Notes

**Date:** July 11, 2026
**Question:** Can we use a teacher LLM to judge model A vs model B completions as a better eval than CSR?

## TL;DR

Your instinct is correct and well-supported by the literature. **Pairwise LLM-as-a-judge** (Zheng et al., 2023, NeurIPS) is an established methodology that achieves >80% agreement with human preferences — the same level as human-human agreement. It fills a genuine gap in our evaluation: it tests **generation quality** (the model's own greedy outputs), which neither PPL (probability of gold) nor CSR (exact-match keystroke savings) measures.

**However**, there are two critical caveats for Basque:
1. **Basque is low-resource** → LLM judges are significantly less reliable on it (Fu et al., 2025). We MUST validate the judge against our existing 30 human A/B judgments before trusting it to scale.
2. **Known biases** (position, verbosity, self-enhancement) must be mitigated with standard techniques.

---

## The Foundational Paper

**Zheng et al. (2023), "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena"** (NeurIPS 2023 Datasets & Benchmarks Track, 10,873 citations).

Key findings:
- GPT-4 as judge achieves **>80% agreement** with both controlled and crowdsourced human preferences — the same level of agreement *between* humans.
- LLM-as-a-judge is a "scalable and explainable way to approximate human preferences, which are otherwise very expensive to obtain."
- Identifies **three biases** and proposes mitigations:

### Bias 1: Position Bias
The judge prefers whichever candidate appears first (or second — it's model-dependent).
**Mitigation:** Swap the order and ask again. Only count a verdict if the judge agrees in both orderings. If it disagrees → tie. This "swap-consistency" filter removes the bias at the cost of reducing effective sample size.

### Bias 2: Verbosity Bias
The judge prefers longer answers, even when explicitly told to ignore length.
**Mitigation:** 
- Explicit instruction ("Do not allow the length of the answer to influence your evaluation") — helps but doesn't fully solve it.
- **Length-controlled debiasing** (Dubois et al., 2024): regression-based adjustment that removes the length effect statistically.
- For autocomplete specifically: truncate both completions to the same token length before judging.

### Bias 3: Self-Enhancement Bias
The judge prefers outputs generated by its own model family.
**Mitigation:** Use a judge from a different model family than the model being evaluated. (Not an issue for us — Morpheus is Mamba-2, no judge is Mamba-2.)

### Additional Finding: Explanations Improve Consistency
Chain-of-thought (judge explains reasoning before giving verdict) improves consistency. Always ask the judge to output a brief justification before the verdict.

### Binary > Graded
Binary judgments (A/B/tie) are more consistent than 1-5 graded scoring (Fu et al., 2025). Fewer options = less noise.

## The Critical Caveat: Multilingual Reliability

**Fu et al. (2025), "How Reliable is Multilingual LLM-as-a-Judge?"** (EMNLP 2025 Findings).

This is the paper that matters most for us. Key findings:

- Evaluated 5 models (GPT-4o, GPT-3.5, Llama-3.3-70B, Qwen-2.5-72B, Aya-Expanse-32B) across 5 tasks and 25 languages.
- **Average Fleiss' Kappa across languages is only ~0.3** (fair to moderate agreement) — far from the ideal of 1.0.
- **Low-resource languages have SIGNIFICANTLY lower consistency**, even for multilingual LLMs specifically designed for cross-lingual performance (Aya-Expanse).
- **Neither multilingual training nor larger model scale directly improves judgment consistency.** Aya-Expanse (purpose-built multilingual) showed the WORST consistency on some tasks.
- GPT-4o achieved the highest consistency but still far from ideal.
- European languages closer to English (Spanish, German) showed higher consistency (~0.3-0.6 Cohen's Kappa); low-resource languages like Arabic and Telugu were near 0.

**Implication for Basque:** Basque is a low-resource isolate. We should expect LLM judges to be unreliable on it. This does NOT mean the approach is useless — it means we MUST validate.

**Mitigation — Ensemble Strategy:** The paper proposes using a majority vote among multiple open-source judges (Llama + Qwen + Aya), which improves consistency over the worst-case single judge. GPT-4o alone is still the best single judge.

## Precedent: LLM Judges on Non-English Generation

**Kocmi & Federmann (2023), "Large Language Models are State-of-the-Art Evaluators of Translation Quality" (GEMBA).**
- Used GPT-4 to evaluate machine translation quality.
- Found GPT-4 matches or beats traditional metrics (BLEU, COMET) on correlation with human judgment.
- WMT23 (the major MT conference) adopted LLM-based metrics as official evaluation.
- **This is the closest published analog to what we want:** using an LLM to judge non-English text generation quality.

## How This Applies to Morpheus

### What Your Proposal Gets Right

The key insight — "give a prompt, follow through the predictions, judge A vs B" — tests **generation quality**: the quality of the model's own greedy outputs. This is a genuinely different evaluation axis from what we have:

| Metric | What it measures | What it misses |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| PPL | Probability the model assigns to gold text | Doesn't test the model's own outputs; a model can have good PPL but poor generation (repetition loops) |
| CSR | Exact-match keystroke savings vs gold | Penalizes valid alternative continuations; saturates; suffers from the CSR paradox |
| **LLM judge (your proposal)** | **Quality of the model's own greedy generation** | **Subjective; biased; unreliable on low-resource languages** |

The LLM judge fills the gap: it can credit valid alternative Basque continuations (which CSR can't) and it tests the model's actual outputs (which PPL doesn't). **This is the right idea.**

### Recommended Design: Reference-Guided Pairwise with Position Swap + Human Validation

**1. Prompts (n=100-200):** Sample from held-out `wiki_valid.txt` (1.83M tokens). We have the gold continuation for each — this is free grounding.

**2. Generation:** For each prompt, each checkpoint (32K, 54K, 74K) generates a greedy continuation (15-30 tokens). Same generation params for all checkpoints.

**3. Judgment (pairwise, reference-guided):**
For each (checkpoint_A, checkpoint_B) pair on each prompt, the judge receives:
- The prompt (prefix)
- The gold continuation (reference — anchors the judgment)
- Continuation A and Continuation B
- Instruction: "Which continuation is more grammatically correct, natural Basque, and more consistent with the reference? Choose A, B, or tie. Do not consider length. Explain briefly, then give your verdict."

**4. Position swap:** Run each pair twice (A-first, B-first). Only count as a win if the judge agrees in both orderings. Disagreement → tie.

**5. Judge selection:**
- **Primary: GPT-4o or Claude** (strongest available, best consistency per the literature).
- **Secondary/validation: Latxa (7B)** as a Basque-native second judge. If GPT-4o and Latxa agree, confidence is higher. (Caveat: 7B may be too weak to judge well — the literature shows multilingual-specific models don't necessarily judge better.)
- **Ensemble:** If using open-source only, majority vote of Llama-3.3 + Qwen-2.5 + Aya.

**6. THE MOST IMPORTANT STEP — Human validation:**
We already have 30 blinded human A/B judgments (§6.6). Run the LLM judge on those EXACT same 30 comparisons and measure agreement:
- If agreement >80% → the judge is trustworthy for Basque; scale to n=300+.
- If agreement 60-80% → the judge is noisy; report with caveats; use it as a secondary signal.
- If agreement <60% → the judge is unreliable for Basque; **this itself is a publishable finding** ("LLM judges are unreliable for low-resource agglutinative language evaluation").

This validation step is the methodological contribution: **calibrating the automated judge against human ground truth before scaling.** It's what makes the approach trustworthy rather than a black box.

### Why Reference-Guided (not reference-free)

Your original proposal was reference-free ("judge which is better"). The literature and our own experience suggest reference-guided is better for autocomplete:
- **Anchoring:** The gold continuation tells the judge what "correct" looks like, reducing subjectivity.
- **Grounding:** The judge isn't just picking its preferred style — it's comparing to what a Basque speaker actually wrote.
- **Consistency with CSR/PPL:** All three metrics use the same held-out gold, making them comparable.
- **Partial credit:** Unlike CSR (exact match only), the judge can credit "this continuation is different from gold but still valid Basque" — the flexibility CSR lacks.

### Bias Controls Checklist
- [x] Position swap (run both orders, consensus only)
- [x] Length control (truncate to same length OR explicit "ignore length" instruction)
- [x] CoT (judge explains before verdict)
- [x] Binary + tie (not 1-5 scale)
- [x] Different model family than Morpheus (any transformer judge works)
- [x] Human validation before scaling

## Risks and Honest Assessment

### Risk 1: Basque is low-resource → judge may be unreliable
This is the #1 risk. Fu et al. (2025) showed low-resource languages have near-zero consistency for some judge-language pairs. **Mitigation:** the human validation step (§6 above) will tell us definitively. If the judge fails validation, we report that as a finding — it's still valuable.

### Risk 2: Checkpoints may produce near-identical greedy outputs
At our quality level (PPL 7.17 vs 7.13), the two checkpoints may generate the same continuation for most prompts. In that case, the judge sees ties — which is a REAL signal (the checkpoints are genuinely close), not a bug. The judge's advantage is that it CAN scale to n=300+ cheaply, giving more statistical power than our n=30 human A/B. But if the true effect size is near zero, even n=300 won't reach significance — and that's an honest result.

### Risk 3: Cost
GPT-4o at n=300 prompts × 3 checkpoint pairs × 2 position swaps = 1,800 API calls. At ~$0.005/call = ~$9. Cheap. Claude similar. This is vastly cheaper than human evaluation.

### Risk 4: The judge may not detect the quality difference that PPL shows
PPL improved 7.17 → 7.13 (small). The judge may not detect this — generation quality may be indistinguishable at this gap. If the judge says "tie" across 300 comparisons, it confirms what CSR already showed: the autocomplete metrics saturate. **This would reinforce our thesis that PPL is the only sensitive metric — but now with a much stronger, well-validated automated metric backing the claim.** Either outcome (judge detects improvement, or judge confirms saturation) is a publishable result.

## Implementation Plan

1. **Extract 100-200 prompts** from `wiki_valid.txt` (we already have CSR prompts — can reuse or sample fresh).
2. **Generate continuations** from 32K, 54K, 74K (greedy, 15-30 tokens, f16 GPU, no BOS — same as CSR eval).
3. **Build judgment prompts** (reference-guided, pairwise, with position swap).
4. **Validate on existing 30 human A/B comparisons** — measure judge-human agreement.
5. **If validated (>80% agreement):** scale to 300+ prompts, run all checkpoint pairs.
6. **Report:** win rates with binomial CIs, swap-consistency rate, judge-human agreement.
